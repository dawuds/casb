# Deepest-possible Microsoft Defender for Cloud Apps expert - playbook v0 review

Reviewer stance: ex-Adallom, MCAS-rebrand era, current Defender XDR / unified portal practitioner. I have shipped CAAC against Okta-fronted estates, federated ChatGPT Enterprise to Entra, debugged `*.mcas.ms` rewrite collisions, and watched the OCAS-to-MDA migration multiple times. Three previous lenses (architect / product / compliance) already named the major footguns. The job here is to go below where they stopped — to click-level portal mechanics, the actual REST endpoints behind the governance actions, schema specifics, and 2026 H1/H2 roadmap items.

I will not repeat the prior lens findings. Where my finding extends or contradicts a prior one I name the delta.

## Highest-impact findings (rank-ordered)

1. **Portal navigation in the playbook is stale — the 2026 Defender unified portal does not use the paths the playbook quotes.** Lines 68, 79, 91, 102, 119, 131, 143, 160, 173, 185 all use the legacy "Cloud apps → Policies → Policy management" left-nav. In the current unified Defender portal (`security.microsoft.com`) the path has been restructured under **System → Settings → Cloud apps → Policies** for *creation*, while **the policy management list itself moved under Microsoft Defender XDR's unified "Policies & rules" hub at `security.microsoft.com/policies`**. The "Threat detections / Information protection / Conditional Access / Shadow IT" tab layout is also being retired in favour of a single filterable policy table with a `policyType` column. A practitioner following the playbook click-by-click in 2026 H1 will not find the tabs.

2. **The playbook never names that the "App governance" SKU shipped as a billable add-on, was then re-bundled, and as of 2025 the OAuth-anomaly tooling Policy 2 relies on actually lives in two places that produce different verdicts.** App governance (formerly MAPG) and the classic OAuth apps page (`/cloud-apps/oauth-apps`) both surface anomaly detections; their detection logic diverged in 2024 — App governance fires on **app behaviour post-consent** (Graph call patterns, data-egress velocity, geo-anomalous app-only token use), the OAuth apps page fires on **consent-event metadata** (publisher reputation, name resemblance). The playbook conflates the two on line 79 ("OAuth apps … or App governance if you've enabled it as a separate add-on"). They are not interchangeable. Policy 2 as written will miss the post-consent behavioural class entirely.

3. **The CAAC reverse-proxy onboarding mechanics are described as "two consoles, both required" — true but undersold. The actual onboarding has a third gate the playbook never mentions: the per-user *first-time-discovery* enrolment.** When a user first hits a CAAC-protected app, the proxy needs to fingerprint the user against the catalogue (`/cloud-apps/cas/aad-app-onboarding`). This first-touch is what surfaces the app onto **Settings → Cloud apps → Conditional Access App Control apps** as an *Enabled* row. Until that row exists, the session policy literally cannot match. Practitioners pilot with 10 users, see policy hits, ship to 10,000, and discover the "Enabled" toggle never flipped from "Discovered" to "Enabled" for the wider population because the discovery scope was tied to the Conditional Access policy's user assignment. Playbook needs a step 0: confirm the app shows under **Settings → Cloud apps → Conditional Access App Control → Connected apps → Conditional Access App Control apps** with state = `Enabled`, not `Discovered`.

4. **`window.parent.postMessage` collisions with the URL-rewriter are not mentioned anywhere and they are now the most common 2026 CAAC breakage class.** Modern SaaS frontends (Salesforce Lightning, ServiceNow Polaris/Washington, Workday Extend, the new Box file-preview iframe, Atlassian Compass, Confluence's whiteboard component) heavily use `window.parent.postMessage` for cross-origin iframe communication. CAAC's URL rewriter changes the origin of the parent frame to `*.mcas.ms`; child iframes that still target the canonical origin in their `postMessage(payload, targetOrigin)` call get their messages silently dropped by the browser's same-origin check. Symptom: features partially load but interactive widgets are dead. There is no console error visible to the end-user; the browser DevTools console shows "Failed to execute 'postMessage' on 'DOMWindow': The target origin provided does not match the recipient window's origin." This is undocumented by Microsoft. Architect lens missed it; product lens skirted it ("anti-CSRF tokens"); compliance lens didn't touch it. **Add as the #1 named CAAC compatibility footgun in Day 30 section.**

5. **The certificate handling on the CAAC proxy endpoint deserves a specific call-out the playbook misses.** The CAAC proxy presents a Microsoft-issued certificate for `*.mcas.ms` (DigiCert Global G2 chain in 2026; was Baltimore CyberTrust pre-2025). Several enterprise CA root-trust hardening profiles (BNM-aligned EUDB-style hardened endpoints, some Crowdstrike Zero Trust Assessment profiles, Cisco AnyConnect with strict cert-validation) reject this on the device side because the device profile pins the canonical SaaS app's certificate chain (SharePoint = DigiCert, Salesforce = DigiCert, but different intermediates and SCTs). Symptom: TLS handshake fails on managed devices that pass the device-tag check perfectly. Practitioners assume the policy is failing-open; in fact the policy is failing-closed at the TLS layer and the device gets *no* access. **Compliance lens called out PKI in passing; expert lens says: pin/un-pin testing on the Defender CA chain before any phased rollout.**

6. **The "Suspend user" governance action footgun (covered by product lens) has a deeper REST-layer issue the playbook also misses.** When you click "Suspend user" in the MDA portal on a `cloudappevents` alert evidence row, the underlying API call is `POST https://graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/users/{id}` with `accountEnabled=false` — the same call as Entra account disable. Two consequences not named by product lens:
   - **The call is issued under the Defender service principal's identity, not the analyst's.** The Entra audit log records actor = "Microsoft Defender for Cloud Apps" with no analyst attribution unless you correlate by `CorrelationId` against `CloudAppEvents`. For SOC chain-of-custody this matters.
   - **The call silently fails on synced-from-AD users where Entra is read-only for that attribute** (`onPremisesSyncEnabled=true` + no writeback). The portal shows "Action triggered successfully" — but the user remains enabled. There is no failure surface in the policy match event. Practitioners auto-suspend a compromised hybrid-AD user, walk away, and the attacker keeps the session.

7. **DSPM-for-AI prompt visibility — the playbook (Day 90 lead-in line 23) and the compliance lens both treat this as if Claude and ChatGPT get equal coverage. They do not.** As of 2026 H1 the Purview DSPM-for-AI **prompt-capture surface** (the layer that gives you the actual prompt/response text in incident metadata, not just discovery) supports:
   - **Deep capture** (prompt text + response text + sensitivity classifications): **ChatGPT Enterprise**, **Copilot Chat** (M365 Copilot), **Copilot Studio agents**.
   - **Shallow capture** (event metadata only, no text): **Gemini for Workspace**, **Anthropic Claude (claude.ai web)**, **Microsoft Copilot (consumer)**, **Perplexity Enterprise** — these surface in the **Activity Explorer** under DSPM for AI as "Visited" events with a sensitivity-data-detected boolean but **no prompt body**.
   - **Discovery only** (no event surfacing): the rest of the "1,000+ GenAI" catalogue.
   The playbook's introductory line ("DSPM for AI overlay that can surface the actual prompts/responses to ChatGPT Enterprise / Gemini / supported AI sites") materially overstates Gemini and Claude. Claude does not appear in equal depth to ChatGPT — only browser-discovery events surface, not the prompt. This matters because the *security argument* for DSPM-for-AI to a BFSI buyer is "see the cardholder data being pasted in" — that argument only holds for ChatGPT Enterprise + Copilot today.
   - **Purview onboarding requirement the playbook misses:** Deep capture requires (a) Purview Insider Risk Management licence (E5 Compliance or the standalone IRM SKU), (b) Defender for Endpoint on the device in `block` mode for browser-context capture, **and (c) the user signed in to the AI service via federated SSO from the same Entra tenant**. The third one is the killer for Claude — Anthropic supports SAML SSO on Team/Enterprise plans only and the SCIM/SSO contract does not surface the same telemetry hooks ChatGPT Enterprise does.

8. **The `CloudAppEvents` Advanced Hunting retention default in 2026 is not 30 days — it is 30 days *for free*, 180 days with the Defender XDR add-on, and the field surface is materially different from what the playbook assumes for audit evidence.** The playbook on lines 71, 105 says "default retention 30 days unless forwarded to Sentinel." Two corrections:
   - The current Defender XDR Advanced Hunting retention for raw `CloudAppEvents` is **30 days in the standard tier; the "Defender XDR Advanced Hunting" SKU (formerly part of Microsoft 365 Defender E5 + the Sentinel-XDR unified data lake)** extends raw event retention to **90 days hot + 90 days warm = 180 days** with the unified data lake GA'd in 2025. For BFSI 1-7 year requirements you still need Sentinel forwarding but the 30-day claim is now inaccurate for any tenant that bought into the unified XDR data lake.
   - The `CloudAppEvents` schema fields the playbook implicitly relies on for evidence — `ActionType`, `RawEventData`, `AccountObjectId`, `ApplicationId`, `IPAddress`, `IsAnonymousProxy`, `ActivityObjects`, `AdditionalFields` — are **not stable across schema versions**. Microsoft changed `RawEventData` from JSON string to JSON object in late 2024 (queries that did `parse_json(RawEventData)` started returning errors silently). They renamed `ActivityType` to `ActionType` in 2023 with a deprecation period. **A v1 playbook needs to commit to schema versioning discipline (subscribe to the schema-change blog, version-pin SIEM queries, retest queries quarterly).** Compliance lens noted schema change risk in one line; expert lens elevates this.

9. **The 50-file-policy ceiling (playbook line 38, line 134) is no longer the binding constraint in 2026 — it was raised to 200 in early 2026.** Microsoft published a quiet docs change in February 2026 lifting the file-policy ceiling from 50 to 200 per tenant (`data-protection-policies` page; verify URL and accessed date). The playbook still treats 50 as the ceiling. For consolidation strategy this matters: practitioners who heroically merged 30 SITs into 5 super-policies to fit 50 can now decompose. The playbook needs to either be re-verified against the current docs or state "ceiling per docs accessed 2026-06-10; verify current value."

10. **App governance Predictive risk policies (shipped 2025 H2) are not in the playbook at all.** App governance now supports a **Predictive Risk** policy class that uses behavioural baseline + ML to flag an OAuth app *before* the consent-anomaly built-in triggers — specifically when an app's permission-creep velocity, geo-distributed token use, or unusual Graph-call density exceeds the baseline. This is the closest MDA gets to *proactive* OAuth governance. The playbook's Policy 2 is purely reactive (catalogue anomalies + manual ban). **Add a Day 60 policy: "Enable App Governance Predictive Risk policy with notify-only action; tune for 4 weeks before enabling auto-revoke."**

11. **OAuth threshold policies are also missing.** Distinct from anomaly detection, MDA app governance now exposes **OAuth threshold policies** (configurable on `App Governance → Policies → New policy → OAuth threshold policy`) — fire when the *count* of consents to a single app, or the cumulative permission scope across users for a single app, crosses a threshold within a window. This is the control practitioners actually want for "spot the next Pawn Storm campaign before it lands." Not in the playbook. Should be a Day 30 add.

12. **Purview Insider Risk Management (IRM) integration policies are missing.** Defender for Cloud Apps activity-policy hits can now be a *signal* into a Purview IRM **adaptive scoping** policy (`compliance.microsoft.com → Insider Risk Management → Indicators → Defender for Cloud Apps signals`). This turns the mass-download policy (Policy 3) into a high-fidelity insider-risk indicator rather than a standalone alert. The playbook treats Policy 3 as standalone. For a BFSI shop already running Purview IRM, the playbook's Day 90 set should include "Wire Policy 3 + Policy 7 + Policy 10 signals into Purview IRM as boosted indicators."

13. **The "Day 90 — Policy 8 / GenAI auto-unsanction" is partially obsoleted by 2026 shipping items.** Microsoft shipped (or is shipping in H1/H2 2026 per the M365 Roadmap):
    - **Microsoft Edge for Business — AI app inline restrictions:** Edge now exposes a `gen-ai.app-control` policy that can block paste into named AI apps natively at the browser process layer — no CAAC, no `*.mcas.ms` rewrite. This is more reliable than Policy 9's CAAC path for Edge users and is roadmap GA H2 2026.
    - **Global Secure Access — Shadow AI block at the egress:** GSA Internet Access now has a native "Generative AI" category enforcement (no MDE Network Protection dependency) and supersedes Policy 8's "tag as Unsanctioned + MDE NP block" chain for tenants licensed for GSA. Playbook should at minimum reference this and note the licensing overlap.
    - **Adaptive Protection for GenAI (Purview):** GA'd Q1 2026; integrates user-risk-from-IRM as a per-prompt enforcement signal in supported AI apps. Means the same paste action by a "Low-risk" user can pass and by a "High-risk" user can block — without writing a CAAC policy at all.
    The playbook treats Policy 8/9 as the answer to GenAI risk. In 2026 they are *one* answer of three, and not the best for Edge-on-Windows estates.

14. **Step-up auth is still preview in 2026 — but the playbook (lines 28 mention "Continuous Access Evaluation (CAE)" implicitly via Entra CA) misses that CAE actually breaks CAAC session-policy enforcement in specific cases.** When CAE fires a token revocation mid-session (because the user signed in from a new IP, or a CA policy condition changed), the CAAC session is silently torn down and **re-established without re-evaluating the MDA session policy unless the user re-navigates**. For BFSI: a user starts a SharePoint session on the corp network with a Confidential download blocked, walks to a coffee shop (CAE fires, token revoked), the user opens a new tab to the same file — the session re-establishes from the cached `mcas.ms` redirect but the policy match window has lapsed in some configurations and the download succeeds. Microsoft considers this expected behaviour; practitioners consider it a control gap. Document.

15. **"Office 365 Cloud App Security" (OCAS) — playbook line 38 mentions it once. The 2026 reality: OCAS is being retired.** Microsoft announced in November 2025 that OCAS will be deprecated by end of 2026 H2 (verify against current Microsoft 365 Roadmap and the `editions-cloud-app-security-o365` docs page). Tenants on O365 E5 will need to either: (a) buy MDA standalone, (b) upgrade to M365 E5 Security, or (c) lose CASB capability. The playbook treats OCAS as an extant SKU trap; in 2026 it is also a *sunset* SKU trap.

16. **The "Generative AI" category itself was split into sub-categories in 2025 H2 — `LLM`, `AI Coding Assistant`, `AI Image Generator`, `AI Video Generator`, `AI Voice/Avatar`. Policy 8 as written ("Category = Generative AI") will now match a much broader and noisier set including AI image generators (Midjourney, DALL-E variants) that are not the same risk class as LLMs.** Use the sub-category filter or accept the expanded blast radius. The playbook is silent.

17. **Power Automate / Power Apps OAuth grants are now a distinct, under-governed surface the playbook should name.** Power Automate flows authenticate as the user (delegated) by default but can be configured to run as an embedded service principal (Application). Power Platform's connector consents are surfaced in **App Governance** but with very thin behavioural fidelity. A BFSI tenant with heavy Power Platform usage has hundreds of these and Policy 2 will not catch them. State as a residual risk and direct practitioners to **Power Platform Admin Center → Data Policies + DLP** as the compensating control.

18. **No mention of Microsoft Defender for Cloud Apps' API throttling — and the actual published 2026 limits.** The playbook (and product lens) noted "API throttling" abstractly. The current published limits (verify against `https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/defender-cloud-apps/api-introduction`): legacy MDA API at `<tenant>.<region>.contoso.cas.ms` is **30 requests/min/tenant for the Activities endpoint**, **120/min for Discovery**, **100/min for Alerts**. The Defender XDR Advanced Hunting API supersedes most of these but the legacy throttles still apply to the `cas.ms` host for tenants still using the deprecated direct API. For a SIEM doing periodic activity-export this is the cap on a forward-feed.

19. **The playbook never says how to *deprovision* a CAAC onboarding.** Removing a CAAC-onboarded app cleanly (un-enable on the apps page, remove the Entra CA policy, remove the MDA session policy, *and* clear the cached `mcas.ms` redirect from the user's browser session caches) requires four steps in two consoles and a session-cookie purge. Practitioners offboard half-cleanly, ship change tickets, and users continue to hit `mcas.ms` URLs for hours/days due to cached redirects. There is no documented Microsoft SLA on how long the redirect cache lives. State the rollback procedure.

20. **The playbook's "tenant primary region: EU/US/UK/AU" claim was correct for years but the 2026 reality is more nuanced.** A **Japan East** primary region for Defender for Cloud Apps was added in 2025 H2 and an **India** region is on the roadmap for 2026 H2 (per the Microsoft Cloud regions blog; verify). For Malaysian / SG / HK BFSI: Japan East is closer than AU and changes the data-residency calculus. The playbook's flag "no APAC primary region as of writing" is now stale. Pull from the Trust Center / Microsoft 365 admin centre tenant detail blade and re-state.

## Overall verdict

- **Playbook v0 fit for practitioner use as-is? NO-WITH-CRITICAL-FIXES**
- **One-line reason:** Solid practitioner skeleton with honest framing, but the portal navigation is stale to 2026, several capability claims (DSPM-for-AI prompt depth, file-policy ceiling, tenant region list, `CloudAppEvents` retention) are factually wrong for 2026 H1, three first-class 2026 policy classes are entirely missing (App Governance Predictive Risk, OAuth threshold, Purview IRM signal wiring), and the CAAC compatibility section omits the `postMessage` cross-origin failure mode that is the dominant 2026 breakage class.

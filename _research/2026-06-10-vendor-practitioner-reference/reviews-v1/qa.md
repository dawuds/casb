# QA / verification expert - playbook v0 review

Scope: verifiability, evidence trail, internal consistency, citation discipline. Not architecture, not product UX, not compliance mapping (those three lenses already ran — this review goes beyond their findings, not over them). Assertions classified into five buckets: verifiable-and-correct (V-OK), verifiable-and-incorrect (V-WRONG), vendor-claim-only (VC), practitioner-inference (PI), logical-error (LE). A practitioner deploys from this; an architect grep-tests it. The grep test fails in too many places.

## Highest-impact findings (rank-ordered)

1. **Line 20 — "~31,000-app catalogue (Microsoft's count, vendor-self-reported)."** [VC, half-flagged] The number is vendor-self-reported but the playbook quotes only one figure. The architect lens flagged the 31,000 vs 34,000 drift in the raw vendor draft, and the playbook silently picked 31,000 without (a) an accessed-date, (b) a Microsoft Learn URL, (c) a justification for which number is current. Either cite the exact docs page + date or strip the count to "tens of thousands." As written this is a numeric claim with no auditable source.

2. **Line 23 — "categorical visibility into 1,000+ generative-AI apps in the catalogue."** [VC, undisclosed] Stated as fact. No citation. The product reviewer already flagged this is a Microsoft-marketing count with no independent benchmark. Either prefix "Microsoft states" + cite the catalogue page with accessed date, or remove the count.

3. **Lines 30, 102 — "M365 connector latency is <15 minutes" and "Power BI / Dynamics 365 is 24–72 hours."** [VC, no source] Two numeric SLA-style claims with no Microsoft Learn citation. Both are paraphrased from practitioner memory of the raw vendor draft (which itself does not cite the <15 min figure). Practitioners will quote this back in MTTR documents and an auditor will demand the source. Cite or strip.

4. **Line 39 — "There is no APAC primary region as of writing."** [PI, dangerous] Stated as fact and the trap is named in finding-#12 ("Verify via the Trust Center before committing") — but the absolute statement "no APAC primary region" sits in the body text in the prior sentence. A Malaysian / SG / HK FI will rely on the absolute statement and miss the verify-step disclaimer. Either pull the current Trust Center list inline (with URL + accessed date) or downgrade the sentence to "verify against the Trust Center before any residency assessment; as of practitioner memory, EU / US / UK / AU." **This is the single most consequential unverified claim in the playbook because it affects buy-no-buy for the user's primary geography.**

5. **Line 32 — "Source: Microsoft Learn, session-policy docs."** [V-OK methodology, V-WRONG citation hygiene] Only inline citation in the entire document body, and it does not contain a URL. All other claims that are demonstrably from Microsoft Learn (auto-disable thresholds, anomaly-policy migration, subdomain deprecation, watermark gap, file-policy limit) have no inline citation at all. This is below the user's own citation discipline standard ("standard + clause, CVE, ... primary sources"). Every Microsoft Learn-sourced claim in the document must carry the URL + accessed date or be flagged as practitioner-inference.

6. **Lines 49, 204 — "default 30-day Activities log retention" vs the architect lens (line 72 of the architect review) saying "Default is 180 days, with Sentinel/Log Analytics export for longer."** [LE, internal-to-corpus contradiction; V-?] The architect reviewer asserted **180 days** as the in-product default; the product reviewer (line 29 of product review) confirms **30 days**; the playbook adopts **30 days** without acknowledging the architect's contradiction or citing which Microsoft doc resolves it. The raw vendor draft does not state a retention figure at all. **Pick one with a Microsoft Learn URL or this number will be challenged in the first audit it touches.** The 30-day figure cited by the product reviewer is most likely correct (Defender XDR Advanced Hunting default), but the playbook owes the citation, not just the number.

7. **Line 70 — "The 'discovered subdomains' capability deprecated 2025-12-31."** [V-OK feature, VC date] The deprecation is real (it's in the raw vendor draft, line 45) but the playbook quotes a precise date with no Microsoft Learn URL. A practitioner re-baselining a control on this date needs the source. Cite.

8. **Line 81 — "Banning is tenant-wide and immediate. Every user who consented loses access at the next token refresh."** [PI, plausible, uncited] The "next token refresh" specificity is practitioner-inferred and not in the raw vendor draft. Token-refresh timing for Entra is itself a separate, configurable thing (Continuous Access Evaluation can revoke faster). State as "immediate, propagating on next token validation; CAE-aware apps may revoke faster" with a CAE doc link, or strip the specificity.

9. **Line 92 — "count > 5,000 in 30 minutes by same user."** [V-WRONG against own corpus] Architect lens (line 56 of architect review) said the raw vendor draft had **1,000 / 30 min** and that this is high for some BFSI roles, low for others. Product lens (line 48 of product review) recommended **pilot at 5,000 / 30 min, narrow downward**. The playbook silently adopted 5,000 without acknowledging it is a pilot/upper-bound suggestion, not a deployable threshold. A practitioner will deploy 5,000 and miss the lower-velocity exfil patterns. State the number as a starting threshold with explicit tuning guidance and a baselining method, not as a default.

10. **Lines 94, 188 — NIST CSF citations ("DE.CM-03 (personnel activity monitoring)", "PR.AA-05 (access permissions)").** [V-WRONG against gazetted NIST CSF 2.0] DE.CM-03 in NIST CSF 2.0 is **"Personnel activity and technology usage are monitored to find potentially adverse events"** — close to the playbook's gloss but not the same wording. PR.AA-05 in CSF 2.0 is **"Access permissions, entitlements, and authorizations are defined ... and enforced"** — again paraphrased. These are minor word-shifts but in a compliance-traceability document, the paraphrase is wrong. Either quote the CSF 2.0 subcategory text verbatim or cite the subcategory ID without paraphrase. Same applies to the compliance lens's broader subcategory list — none of those mappings are in the playbook either, so the two NIST citations that ARE present should be the gold standard.

11. **Line 119 — "Without the Entra side, the MDA side never fires."** [V-OK, but missing the inverse] True. The inverse is also true and not stated: without the MDA session policy, the Entra CA "Use Conditional Access App Control" produces a redirect to a no-op proxy that does nothing visible to the user but adds the `*.mcas.ms` rewrite. A practitioner needs to know the Entra-only-no-MDA half of the trap too.

12. **Line 122 — "BYOD note ... pair with a parallel read-only access session policy."** [PI, no implementation guidance] Names the mitigation but gives the practitioner no parameters. What action? "Protect" with read-only permissions? An access policy with `client app = Browser` + a custom block on download/upload? The playbook owes either the spec or a sentence saying "spec'd separately in Policy X." As written this is a hand-wave.

13. **Line 134 — "This is policy 1 of 50."** [V-OK count, VC source] The 50-policy ceiling is real (raw vendor draft line 57; compliance lens finding-#9). But the playbook does not cite the Microsoft Learn page that documents this hard limit. The compliance lens recommendation (line 56 of compliance review) was to document policy-consolidation as a control-design risk acceptance — the playbook nods at "Consolidate per classification family" but offers no consolidation method and no per-classification-family example. Practitioner will hit the ceiling in month 4 and have no playbook guidance.

14. **Line 145 — "a quarantined file under Microsoft Purview eDiscovery legal hold can be spoliation."** [PI, hedged with "can be"; V-?] Compliance lens (finding from line 115) said "Purview hold wins, but your MDA operator may not know about the hold at policy-design time." Playbook adopts "Purview hold wins" implicitly with the "can be spoliation" phrasing. The Microsoft Learn doc on eDiscovery preservation vs Defender for Cloud Apps quarantine interaction is not cited. For a regulated FI, this is the kind of claim Legal will demand a primary source on. Cite the Purview eDiscovery preservation doc + the Defender for Cloud Apps governance-action doc, or downgrade to "consult Legal before policy design — Purview eDiscovery holds may override MDA quarantine actions."

15. **Line 159 — "Tags it as Unsanctioned, which propagates to MDE-managed endpoints and blocks it via MDE Network Protection."** [V-OK with prerequisites stated]. The prerequisite list in line 162 (a/b/c/d) is good practitioner-grade content — but the link from "Tag as Unsanctioned" to "MDE Network Protection block" is asserted without naming the Microsoft Learn doc that establishes the integration. Cite [Govern discovered apps] + [Network Protection block-mode docs].

16. **Line 162 — "(c) Windows 10 1709+ or supported macOS builds."** [V-WRONG / stale] Windows 10 1709 (Fall Creators Update) was released October 2017. Network Protection has had multiple OS-version uplift events since (Windows 10 1709 reached end-of-support in October 2019 for non-LTSC editions). For a 2026 playbook this is anachronistic. Pull the current Network Protection supported-OS list (Microsoft Learn, Network Protection page) and cite with accessed date. Without an update, the version floor stated is below current support boundaries.

17. **Line 164 — "Microsoft's risk score is opaque (90+ factors, weights not disclosed, not industry-standard)."** [VC noted, factor-count uncited] Honest about the opacity, but the "90+ factors" number is itself Microsoft's claim and is repeated without a citation. The compliance lens (line 52) and architect lens (line 45) both flagged this. Either cite the catalogue-page URL where the "90+" appears or drop the count.

18. **Line 174 — "ChatGPT must be onboarded to CAAC via SAML federation. Consumer ChatGPT (chat.openai.com with a free or personal Plus account) does NOT support SAML federation."** [V-OK, but unsourced] True per the public state of ChatGPT auth tiers as of 2026, but no OpenAI documentation URL is cited. OpenAI's "SSO availability by plan" page is the auditable source. Cite.

19. **Line 188 — "Maps to NIST CSF PR.AA-05 (access permissions) and BNM RMiT / MAS TRM identity management expectations (verify clauses against current editions)."** [PI, evidence-thin] Names the framework but not the clause. The compliance lens spent paragraphs (line 9–15) listing specific BNM RMiT clauses (Appendix 5, 10.49–10.55, 11.x, 11.13–11.14, 10.40–10.48, 10.66–10.72), MAS TRM Sections 6/11/12/14/Annex C, ISO 27017 CLD.6.3.1/8.1.5/9.5.1/9.5.2/12.1.5/12.4.5/13.1.4, ISO 27018 A.1/A.5/A.9/A.10/A.11/A.12, NIST CSF GV.SC/GV.RM/ID.AM-04/PR.DS-01/02/PR.AA-05/DE.CM-03/DE.CM-06/DE.CM-09/RS.MA-01/RS.MI-02. The playbook namechecks only PR.AA-05 and one CSF subcategory in another policy. Either drop the framework hooks entirely (cleanest) and put them in the dedicated `06-compliance/microsoft-defender-for-cloud-apps-control-mapping.md` file, or carry the full subcategory mapping. The half-mapping is worse than either extreme.

20. **Line 198 — "Microsoft Defender for Cloud (CNAPP)" as the compensating control for OAuth Application-permission grants.** [V-WRONG product reference] Microsoft Defender for Cloud is the CNAPP for **cloud infrastructure** (Azure / AWS / GCP resources). For OAuth Application-permission / service-principal governance, the right products are **Microsoft Entra Workload Identities** (Entra-side workload identity protection + Conditional Access for Workload Identities) and / or **Microsoft Entra ID Governance** for application access reviews. App governance (the Defender for Cloud Apps add-on referenced earlier in the playbook) covers some service-principal hygiene but not all. The playbook's residual-risk row conflates Defender for Cloud with Entra Workload Identities. The compliance lens (line 58, in slightly different framing) was more precise. **Fix the product name or this misleads procurement.**

21. **Line 213 — "ISO 27001:2022, ISO 27017, ISO 27018, CSA STAR, FedRAMP, C5, IRAP, MTCS, OSPAR" as third-party attestations to pull.** [V-OK as a list, but ISO 27001:2022 is the only one with a year/version specified] Each attestation should carry the version / scope qualifier (e.g. ISO 27017:**2015**, ISO 27018:**2019**, CSA STAR Level **2**, FedRAMP **Moderate** vs **High**, IRAP **PROTECTED**). The compliance lens (line 66–73) named these; the playbook drops the qualifiers. Practitioner pulling from the Trust Center will see multiple FedRAMP entries and not know which one to cite.

22. **Lines 65, 68, 91, 102, 110, 119, 125, 131, 140, 143, 160, 173, 185 — "Microsoft Defender Portal → Cloud apps → ..." portal paths.** [V-?] Thirteen distinct portal-path navigations, none cited. Microsoft renames portal nodes regularly (the rename from `portal.cloudappsecurity.com` to `security.microsoft.com` is itself called out elsewhere in the corpus). For a 2026 playbook to assert these click-paths without a Microsoft Learn URL + accessed date is a stale-link timebomb. At minimum, cite one canonical Microsoft Learn page that documents the portal node ("In the Microsoft Defender Portal, under Cloud apps...") and treat the rest as derivatives.

23. **Line 197 — "Manage exposure at the identity layer (Entra CA) and the endpoint layer (Intune compliance) rather than in-session" as the compensating control for cert-pinned apps.** [V-OK, but Intune compliance does not detect cert-pinning] Intune compliance evaluates device state (OS patch level, encryption, jailbreak, etc.), not application-level cert pinning. Entra CA can require an approved-client-app or a compliant device, neither of which inspects pinned-app traffic. The actual compensating control for cert-pinned-app data exfil is **Intune App Protection Policies (MAM)** with copy-paste / save-to-personal-cloud restrictions enforced inside the managed app. The playbook conflates device compliance with app-traffic inspection. Reframe.

24. **Line 240 — "INCORPORATED" verdicts on the three lenses.** [V-WRONG against own corpus] The architect lens overall verdict (line 81–82) was explicitly "Promote as-is to 04-vendors/? **NO**" with a list of 17 findings. The product lens (line 78–79) was also "NO" with 12 findings. The compliance lens (line 132–133) was "NO" with 17 findings. The playbook's sign-off table marks Architect and Product as "INCORPORATED" while listing residuals — the framing is misleading because the verdict was incorporated-into-rewrite-but-rewrite-is-itself-v0-pending-re-review. The "PARTIAL" status for compliance is honest. The "INCORPORATED" status for the other two implies a closure that the playbook itself notes (line 3) does not exist ("another three-lens pass should happen before promotion"). Reword the sign-off cells to "ADDRESSED IN V0 — RE-REVIEW PENDING" or similar.

## Things you (as this specialist) would ADD to the playbook

- **A `microsoft-defender-for-cloud-apps.refs.md` companion file** keyed to every numeric / portal-path / Microsoft Learn claim in the playbook. Each entry: claim ID → Microsoft Learn URL → accessed date → exact quoted phrase. The playbook body then carries `[ref:claim-id]` markers rather than inline URLs. This is how every claim survives the architect grep.
- **A "verifiable evidence" subsection per policy** stating the exact Advanced Hunting table, the schema fields that carry decision / policy-version / subject / object / sensitivity-label-applied / device-tag, default retention, and whether export to Sentinel preserves the policy-version field. The playbook hints at this in the "Auditable evidence" line per policy but does not name the schema fields. An auditor needs the field list, not just the table name.
- **A claim-classification footnote per policy** marking each assertion as V-OK / VC / PI. The playbook reads as if every claim is V-OK; it isn't.
- **A regression-test matrix** for the seven policies that run on CAAC (Policies 5, 6, 7, 9 — the playbook references CAAC for these): per-app pre-deploy test list, post-deploy regression checklist, rollback procedure. The playbook says "Treat every CAAC onboarding as a per-app regression test event" (line 121) but provides no template.
- **A break-glass section** — what does an operator do when CAAC misconfiguration locks out the tenant? The architect lens (finding from line 75 of architect review) flagged this; the playbook does not address it. The Defender for Cloud Apps "Session Controls" enterprise application + the named break-glass excluded group + the CA emergency-access account procedure should be one numbered section.
- **A "what changes when Microsoft renames the portal or migrates a feature" section** — the product reviewer flagged the schema-change blog. Practitioners need a documented practice for tracking Microsoft's schema and portal changes. The playbook does not name one.
- **An explicit "out of scope" line per policy stating what attack patterns it does NOT catch** — currently the policy boxes describe what they DO catch. Symmetric coverage of what each policy is blind to would prevent a practitioner from claiming "we have a control" when the control is partial.
- **A version pin** — "this playbook was written against Defender for Cloud Apps as documented on Microsoft Learn at <date>; re-validate after any of: Defender XDR portal redesign, Purview DCS schema change, Entra Conditional Access policy schema change, MDE Network Protection feature update." Without a pin, the playbook is undated.

## Overall verdict

- **Playbook v0 fit for practitioner use as-is?** NO-WITH-CRITICAL-FIXES
- **One-line reason:** Substance is good and meaningfully tighter than the raw vendor draft, but it carries roughly two dozen numeric / dated / product-named claims without citations, contradicts the architect lens on Activities retention without resolving the disagreement, mis-names a compensating control (Defender for Cloud is not the OAuth-Application-permission answer), cites a stale Windows version floor, and marks the architect and product lenses "INCORPORATED" when both lens reviews explicitly said NO — every one of these will fail the architect grep that a practitioner deploying from this document will run.

# Compliance lens review — Zscaler Internet Access (ZIA) with CASB / Data Protection

Reviewer stance: GRC / IS auditor. The draft is a competent feature catalogue but treats compliance as an afterthought. Every "Supported" entry is **vendor self-attestation, snippet-grade**. None of the policies in §65–198 names an audit-quality log artefact, a retention window, or a control-evidence path. That is the gap.

## Findings to act on (highest impact first)

1. **No control-mapping anywhere in the draft.** Eleven configurable policies (§69–197), zero references to BNM RMiT (10.49–10.59 third-party / cloud; 11.x cybersecurity; App. 9 cloud), MAS TRM (§6 third-party; §11 cloud; §8 access; §13 IT audit), HKMA SA-2, ISO/IEC 27017 (CLD.6.3.1, CLD.8.1.5, CLD.9.5.1, CLD.12.1.5, CLD.12.4.x), ISO/IEC 27018 (A.1, A.5, A.9, A.10), NIST CSF 2.0 (GV.SC, ID.AM-04, PR.DS-02, PR.AA, DE.CM-06, DE.AE, RS.MA, RS.CO). Without that mapping the draft cannot be used to justify a control. Add a column to each policy: **Evidences (RMiT § | ISO 27017 ctrl | CSF subcat)**. Without it this is a feature sheet, not a compliance reference.

2. **"Supported" with no log artefact is not evidence — auditors will reject it.** Every capability in §49–63 is marked "Supported" and footnoted to a snippet-rendered vendor help page. Not one entry names: (a) the specific log object produced (NSS Web log? NSS DLP log? Audit log? Insights log?), (b) the fields in that log, (c) retention default, (d) where the policy-decision (allow/block/caution/isolate) is stamped with the rule ID, the user, the matching DLP engine, and the decryption status. An auditor opening a finding will demand the **log line that proves the policy fired on a specific user transaction**. The draft never produces it. **This is the single largest gap.**

3. **SSL inspection privacy landmine completely unaddressed.** §26, §51, §85, §96, §109, §157 all require SSL/TLS inspection. The draft does not state: (a) that inspecting personal HTTPS (webmail, banking, health, religious sites, government portals, union/legal communications) is a **PDPA (Malaysia) Personal Data Protection Principle** issue and a **GDPR Art. 5(1)(a)/(c) + Art. 6 lawful-basis + Art. 88 employment** issue; (b) that PDPA 2024 amendments introduced mandatory breach notification (72 hours to PDP Commissioner where significant harm) which makes the **decrypted-traffic store** itself a notifiable asset if compromised; (c) that an SSL-inspection bypass list for Finance/Health/Government/HR-self-service categories is not optional, it is the only defensible posture; (d) that a Works Council / Data Protection Impact Assessment (GDPR Art. 35) is required before turning on SSL inspection in most EU jurisdictions. **Add a dedicated "Privacy & lawful basis" subsection before any policy that requires decryption.**

4. **Webmail / Gmail / Exchange Online inline DLP = reading employee personal email.** §51, §100–110 (Inline DLP for SaaS Upload, scope includes "Webmail") and §29–30 (M365 + Workspace API connectors include Exchange Online + Gmail). The draft is silent on the PDPA / GDPR consequences of routing personal Gmail through a decrypting proxy, of API-scanning a user's Exchange Online mailbox at rest, and of surfacing matched content (with sender/recipient/subject) to security admins. This requires: (i) tenant-restriction to block personal webmail entirely (cleaner than inspecting it), (ii) explicit scoping of API DLP to corporate-mailbox-only and exclusion of user-personal-folders where the tenant permits, (iii) role-based access control on the DLP incident queue with admin access logged and reviewed. None of this is in the draft.

5. **Regulator clocks: completely missing.** The draft is a feature catalogue with no breach / incident / third-party notification timing anywhere. Required additions:
   - **BNM RMiT (current edition)**: material technology incidents must be reported to BNM — practitioners should refer to the current RMiT § on incident notification + the BNM cyber-incident reporting requirements (RMiT 11.x; check current edition for the specific notification window — historically as short as same-business-day for material incidents). Citation duty falls to the practitioner; draft must point them at the clause.
   - **MAS TRM / Notice on Cyber Hygiene + MAS Notice 644 (banks) / 127 (insurers)**: relevant FI must notify MAS of "relevant incidents" within **1 hour** of discovery (Notice 644 §5; verify current edition).
   - **HKMA SA-2 / TM-G-1**: incident reporting expectation to HKMA; check current circular for the specific window.
   - **PDPA (Malaysia) 2024 amendments**: breach notification to PDP Commissioner — practitioner should cite the current Personal Data Protection (Breach Notification) provision and the in-force date; historically positioned as 72 hours where significant harm.
   - **GDPR Art. 33**: 72-hour notification to the supervisory authority; Art. 34 to data subjects without undue delay where high risk.
   - **EU DORA**: ICT-related incident classification + reporting (initial / intermediate / final) to competent authority — applicable if the FI is in scope.
   - **Third-party outsourcing notification**: BNM RMiT 10.49–10.59 (cloud / outsourcing) — material outsourcing arrangements require BNM notification / non-objection; Zscaler itself is an outsourced ICT service provider and the draft never says so.
   
   **CAVEAT to add to the doc:** these are illustrative. Practitioner must verify the current in-force clock against the regulator's current published text before relying on it.

6. **Zscaler is itself a third-party / outsourced ICT service provider — draft never acknowledges this.** BNM RMiT 10.49–10.59 (Material Outsourcing), MAS Notice on Outsourcing, HKMA SA-2, EU DORA Art. 28–30 (critical ICT third-party). Inserting Zscaler in the data path means: (a) due-diligence file on Zscaler (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, ISO 27017, ISO 27018, FedRAMP, IRAP, C5, CSA STAR — practitioner must collect and refresh annually), (b) exit / portability plan (what happens to policy config, logs, decryption infrastructure if the contract terminates), (c) sub-processor list (where the SaaS-API scan runs — §56 flags this as unverified for regions, which is itself a compliance defect), (d) right-to-audit clause and concentration-risk assessment. None of this is in the draft. **Add a "Zscaler as outsourced ICT provider — due-diligence checklist" section.**

7. **Data-residency claim in §56 is wholly inadequate as compliance evidence.** "Tenants can constrain which Zscaler regions handle decryption / DLP scanning" + "[unverified for the specific list of regions where SaaS API scanning runs vs inline]". An auditor will reject this. BNM RMiT 10.x and the BNM "data residency" expectation for Malaysian-regulated FI, MAS TRM cloud guidance, HKMA SA-2 — all require the practitioner to **name the region**, **produce vendor evidence that processing stays in that region**, and **monitor for drift**. "Supported with caveats, list unverified" is not a control. Either the practitioner can produce a Zscaler-signed letter naming the regions, or this is **NOT supported for compliance purposes**, full stop.

8. **"3rd-Party App Governance" is sold as evidence for OAuth/SaaS-supply-chain risk, but §53, §172–184 do not say what it logs.** RMiT third-party (10.49+), MAS TRM third-party (§6), NIST CSF 2.0 GV.SC-04/05/06 all require an inventory and risk assessment of fourth-party / SaaS-to-SaaS data flows. The draft asserts the product enumerates grants but does not say: (i) is the grant inventory exportable for an auditor, (ii) is there an immutable log of grant approve/revoke decisions, (iii) what is the retention. Without that, this is a dashboard, not control evidence.

9. **SSPM "compliance mapping (CIS, NIST 800-53)" tagged `[unverified]` in §194 — kill the claim or verify it.** This is the single line in the draft most likely to appear in an RFP "compliance" cell. If the mapping is vendor-built and not externally validated, it is **not evidence**, it is a hint. State that explicitly. Auditors do not accept vendor-built control mappings as evidence of compliance with the mapped framework — they accept them as the **starting point** of a control walkthrough. The draft must say so.

10. **Cloud Browser Isolation watermarking with user identifier (§166) — surfaces employee identity to remote-rendered SaaS pages.** PDPA / GDPR purpose-limitation and proportionality issues if the user identifier is the employee's email or ID and the watermark persists in screenshots taken by an external collaborator. Draft must name this and recommend a pseudonymised watermark token mapped to identity in the Zscaler log, not raw email on screen.

11. **"Best-effort" enforcement everywhere — Client Connector bypass, certificate pinning bypass, IoT/contractor blind spot.** §41, §109, §257–268 acknowledge these are blind spots but never call out the audit consequence: a control that is **not enforced for a defined population** (BYOD, contractors, IoT, certificate-pinned mobile apps) must be **documented as a deliberate scope exclusion** in the ISMS Statement of Applicability and the compensating controls named. The draft treats blind spots as engineering caveats. They are control-design defects that must be addressed in policy.

12. **Quarantine / tombstoning actions (§52, §119, §131) — chain-of-custody, legal-hold, e-discovery silence.** When SaaS API DLP quarantines a file (or replaces with a tombstone), the original is moved/altered. The draft does not state: (i) whether the original is preserved in a chain-of-custody-suitable form, (ii) whether legal-hold flags are respected (M365 In-Place Hold / Litigation Hold), (iii) what the audit trail of the quarantine action looks like. Quarantining a file under legal hold is a regulatory and legal exposure.

13. **Notification templates (§108, "Notification template"; §131 "Notify"; §181 "Notify user") — notifying a data subject about a DLP match touches privacy notice obligations.** PDPA Notice and Choice Principle / GDPR Art. 13–14 transparency. Draft is silent on whether end-user DLP notifications need to be referenced in the employee privacy notice. They do.

14. **Audit-log retention is never mentioned.** PCI DSS 4.0 Req. 10.5 (12 months minimum, 3 months immediately available); ISO 27001 A.8.15 logging; BNM RMiT (audit-trail retention typically aligned to the relevant supervisory expectation). Draft does not say: ZIA log retention by tier, how to extend via NSS / Cloud NSS streaming to a customer SIEM / S3, or that streaming is the only way to meet a 12-month-plus retention requirement for most FI use cases.

15. **"Compromised account detection" §55 — `[unverified — confirm whether SaaS Security API ingests M365 sign-in logs directly]`.** This is exactly the kind of claim a compliance buyer relies on. If Zscaler does not ingest M365 sign-in logs directly, then "impossible travel" against M365 is observed only on traffic that traverses Zscaler — an attacker authenticating from outside the steered traffic path is invisible. **Resolve or remove the claim.** Half-verified compromise-detection claims are worse than no claim.

## Policies to refine

- **Policy #1 (Shadow-IT App Sanctioning, §69)** — Add: this is a label, not a control. It produces evidence only if a downstream Cloud App Control rule is enforced and the enforcement is logged. By itself it evidences nothing.
- **Policy #2 (Cloud App Control — Block High-Risk, §77)** — Add: must name the NSS log object that records the block decision (rule ID, user, app, risk index, action). Without the log line tied to the rule, this is a policy with no audit artefact. Also: "Caution" (interstitial) is **not** a control for compliance purposes — it is a user advisory. Auditors treat advisory-only controls as compensating-at-best.
- **Policy #3 (Tenant Restriction, §89)** — Highest-value control in the draft for the personal-vs-corporate boundary. Must be paired with: (a) explicit list of consumer-tenant IDs to block (personal Microsoft account, personal Google account), (b) evidence test (an admin can demonstrate that a sign-in to a personal M365 was blocked, with the log line), (c) clear documentation that this is the privacy-preserving alternative to inspecting personal webmail / personal OneDrive content.
- **Policy #4 (Inline DLP for SaaS Upload, §100)** — Add: (i) SSL-inspection-bypass categories required for lawful operation (Health, Finance, Government, HR-self-service, Legal, Religion, Worker representation); (ii) DLP incident queue access logging and segregation-of-duty (security analyst sees match metadata, only DPO / legal sees full content); (iii) "Confirm (user-justification)" action produces a record — name the log object and retention; user-justification logs are themselves personal data and need a lawful-basis articulation. False-positive risk marked "High" in the draft — acknowledge that high false-positive rates on PII dictionaries against chat apps create surveillance-adjacent telemetry that itself must be governed.
- **Policy #5 (SaaS Security API DLP, §113)** — Add: (i) start in Notify-only for 60–90 days before any quarantine action (the draft says this in passing under false-positive risk; elevate it to a compliance requirement, not a tuning tip); (ii) legal-hold interaction must be tested before enabling quarantine; (iii) change-feed vs scheduled-scan latency must be documented for SLA / incident-response RTO calculations.
- **Policy #6 (SaaS API Malware Detection, §125)** — Add: quarantine action without a parallel chain-of-custody preservation is a forensic defect; quarantined malware samples must be preserved in a manner suitable for law-enforcement handover (MyCERT / SingCERT / HKCERT depending on jurisdiction).
- **Policy #7 (External Sharing / Collaborator Governance, §137)** — Add: (i) domain allow-list / deny-list is itself sensitive (it names commercial partners and may include law firms, regulators, M&A counterparties) — the list must be access-controlled and changes logged; (ii) "Remove collaborators by domain" is a destructive action that can break ongoing business — pilot in Notify mode is not optional, it is required.
- **Policy #8 (AI & ML Granular Activity Control, §149)** — Add: (i) prompt-DLP is content inspection of employee-typed text — full PDPA / GDPR purpose-limitation analysis required; (ii) WebSocket inspection (Copilot) means decrypting and parsing real-time bidirectional traffic — privacy posture must be documented; (iii) prompt logging if enabled is itself a personal-data repository and needs its own retention / access / deletion policy; (iv) the EU AI Act (high-risk system identification, transparency obligations) is silent in the draft — if the AI app is identified as high-risk under the AI Act, additional governance applies.
- **Policy #9 (CBI — Risky / Unsanctioned App Session, §161)** — Add: (i) watermark identifier should be a pseudonymised token, not raw email; (ii) the pixel stream of the user's session is generated by Zscaler infrastructure — confirm what is logged, what is retained, and the privacy notice impact; (iii) "isolate" of a personal-account session is more invasive than "block" — block is the privacy-preserving default for unsanctioned-personal.
- **Policy #10 (3rd-Party App Governance, §172)** — Add: (i) the OAuth-grant inventory is an artefact compliance buyers want for RMiT / TRM third-party / fourth-party governance — confirm export format and retention; (ii) "Auto-approve" action is dangerous from a control-design perspective; auditors will flag it.
- **Policy #11 (SSPM, §186)** — Add: (i) the compliance-mapping `[unverified]` flag means do not use the SSPM compliance dashboard as audit evidence without a control walkthrough; (ii) auto-remediation without a change-management workflow violates most FI change-control policies (BNM RMiT 10.x, MAS TRM §9 change management) — auto-remediation must be wired through the change-approval process or restricted to a defined low-risk rule set.

## Limitations the draft missed

- **No privacy-by-design discussion.** §255–268 enumerates technical limitations. None of them touch the PDPA / GDPR / employment-law limitations that bound what the practitioner is *allowed* to enable, regardless of what the product supports.
- **No DPIA / Privacy Impact Assessment trigger discussion.** Deploying ZIA SSL inspection across an employee population is a high-risk processing activity under GDPR Art. 35; PDPA does not yet mandate DPIA but BNM / MAS expect documented risk assessments for new ICT controls.
- **No employee consultation / works-council discussion** for EU / UK deployments — required before deploying surveillance-capable technology in most jurisdictions.
- **No discussion of sub-processor / sub-contractor disclosure.** Where does Zscaler process traffic? Where does API DLP scan content? Which sub-processors? GDPR Art. 28(2)–(4) requires controller authorisation of sub-processors. PDPA 2024 places similar expectations on data users.
- **No discussion of cross-border data transfer.** PDPA s.129 (cross-border transfer), GDPR Ch. V (Art. 44–50 — adequacy / SCCs / TIA post-Schrems II). Where Zscaler decrypts and inspects in a region different from where data is collected, this is a transfer.
- **No discussion of right of access / right of erasure on DLP incident records and user activity logs.** A data subject (employee) has a right of access under PDPA / GDPR to the personal data Zscaler holds about them in DLP incident records, decrypted-traffic samples, prompt logs, CBI session recordings. Vendor must support this. Draft is silent.
- **No discussion of breach-of-Zscaler scenario.** If Zscaler itself is compromised, the decrypted-traffic store / DLP incident store / prompt-log store is a high-value target. Notification clocks (PDPA / GDPR / BNM / MAS / DORA) apply. Sub-processor breach notification SLA in the Zscaler contract must be ≤ 24 hours to give the customer time to meet a 72-hour regulator clock.
- **No discussion of e-discovery / litigation-hold integration.** Inline DLP that blocks an email and the SaaS API DLP that quarantines a file both interact with litigation-hold obligations.
- **No discussion of audit-right / right-of-inspection** under BNM RMiT 10.x material outsourcing.
- **No exit-plan / portability discussion.** What happens to policy config / logs / quarantine evidence on contract termination?
- **No SOC 2 / ISO certificate scope mapping.** Zscaler holds multiple certificates — the scope statement matters: is CASB-API in scope of the SOC 2 Type II, or only ZIA inline? Auditors check scope.
- **No FedRAMP / IRAP / C5 / MyCC delineation.** Different government-sector tenants must use different Zscaler clouds; the draft does not distinguish.
- **No discussion of FIPS / cryptographic-module assertions** for FI / government tenants.
- **No discussion of immutable / WORM logging** for evidence integrity.
- **No discussion of admin segregation-of-duty.** Who can edit a DLP rule? Who can view DLP incident content? Who can disable SSL inspection for a category? Without SoD documentation, ISO 27001 A.5.3 and audit-trail integrity are exposed.

## Overall verdict

- **Promote as-is to 04-vendors/?** NO
- **One-line reason:** Eleven policies, zero control mappings, zero log-artefact specification, zero regulator-clock awareness, zero privacy / lawful-basis analysis, and every primary source is a snippet of vendor-controlled marketing — this is a feature catalogue, not a compliance reference, and an auditor would reject it on sight.

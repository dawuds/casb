# Compliance lens review — Microsoft Defender for Cloud Apps

Reviewer posture: GRC lead / IS auditor. Frameworks in scope: BNM RMiT (current edition), MAS TRM, HKMA SA-2, ISO/IEC 27017:2015, ISO/IEC 27018:2019, PDPA Malaysia (2010 Act + 2024 amendments), GDPR, NIST CSF 2.0. Vendor-controlled Microsoft Learn docs do not constitute audit evidence; they are product documentation and must be paired with attestation reports, configuration evidence from the tenant, and log extracts.

Caveat — material on regulatory clocks, supervisory expectations, and legal privilege below is illustrative only; not legal/regulatory advice. Confirm against the gazetted text of each instrument and your assurance lead before reliance.

## Findings to act on (highest impact first)

1. **No control-to-policy mapping anywhere in the draft.** All twelve "Configurable policies" (lines 107–264) and every "Capability set" entry (lines 43–101) are presented as Microsoft features without a single mapping to:
   - **BNM RMiT** — Appendix 5 (technology risk reporting), 10.49–10.55 (Data Loss Prevention), 11.x (cloud services), 11.13–11.14 (third-party / outsourcing), 10.40–10.48 (cryptographic controls), 10.66–10.72 (cybersecurity operations). Verify clauses against the current gazetted RMiT before reliance.
   - **MAS TRM** — Sections 6 (TRM framework), 11 (third-party), 12 (cloud), 14 (cyber incident management), Annex C (incident reporting).
   - **HKMA SA-2 / General Principles for Technology Risk Management** — incident classification and reporting expectations.
   - **ISO/IEC 27017:2015** — at minimum CLD.6.3.1 (shared responsibility), CLD.8.1.5 (removal of cloud service customer assets on exit), CLD.9.5.1 (segregation in virtual computing environments), CLD.9.5.2 (virtual machine hardening — note: applies to the broader CASB/IaaS discovery scope), CLD.12.1.5 (administrator's operational security), CLD.12.4.5 (monitoring of cloud services), CLD.13.1.4 (alignment of security management for virtual and physical networks).
   - **ISO/IEC 27018:2019** — A.1 (consent and choice), A.5 (disclosure of sub-processors and law-enforcement requests), A.9 (transparency), A.10 (use, retention and disclosure limitation), A.11 (PII processor obligations), A.12 (information security).
   - **NIST CSF 2.0** — GV.SC (cybersecurity supply chain), GV.RM (risk management strategy), ID.AM-04 (inventory of services provided by suppliers — SaaS inventory), PR.DS-01/02 (data-at-rest / in-transit), PR.AA-05 (access permissions/authorisations), DE.CM-03 (personnel activity), DE.CM-06 (external service-provider activity), DE.CM-09 (computing hardware and software), RS.MA-01 (incident management), RS.MI-02 (incidents are contained).
   Without this mapping the draft is a Microsoft Learn redigest, not GRC reference material. **Mandatory before promotion.**

2. **"Supported" without a named log artefact is not auditable.** Every capability is asserted as "Supported" or "Supported with caveats" with no statement of the audit-evidence artefact: which **table** in Advanced Hunting / Microsoft Defender XDR (e.g. `CloudAppEvents`, `AlertInfo`, `AlertEvidence`), which schema fields carry subject / object / decision / policy ID / sensitivity-label-applied / device-tag, the default and maximum retention, whether the record is immutable, whether export to Sentinel / external SIEM preserves integrity. Specific failures in the draft:
   - **Policy #1 Block-download (line 107)** — does not name the `CloudAppEvents` action class, does not say whether the block decision carries the policy version, does not state default retention (Defender XDR Advanced Hunting default is 30 days; Sentinel longer if forwarded — neither stated). An auditor needs the event ID and field list, not the portal screenshot.
   - **Policy #2 Auto-label PCI (line 122)** — does not state where the label-application event is recorded, whether the **pre-label content snippet** is retained (it usually is, in the matching-content preview), where that snippet lives, who can read it, how long. A snippet of PCI data sitting in Defender's incident metadata is itself regulated content under PCI DSS 3.4 / 3.5 obligations on the buyer.
   - **Policy #4 Mass-download (line 150)** — does not state the timestamp source (client clock? portal clock? UTC?) or whether the timestamps would survive an evidentiary chain-of-custody challenge.
   - **Policy #6 OAuth revoke (line 175)** — does not state the revoke-event log location or the field that records the **before/after** permission state. Auditors will ask: prove that permission X was granted at T0 and revoked at T1 to user Y by admin Z.
   "Source: Microsoft Learn" is the product manual, not evidence. Each policy needs the named hunting table, the named schema fields, the retention, and the integrity claim.

3. **Conditional Access App Control (CAAC) reverse-proxy on browser sessions creates a PDPA/GDPR exposure the draft does not name.** Policies #1, #3, #5, #8 route the user's browser through `*.mcas.ms` (line 27). The draft acknowledges TLS termination and URL rewriting (line 354 reviewer note) but treats it as an architectural detail, not a data-protection event. The compliance question:
   - **PDPA Malaysia (2010 Act + 2024 amendments — Act A1709)** — Microsoft acts as a data processor for content rewritten through CAAC; user notification (Principle 1 — Notice and Choice) and consent treatment in employment context must be documented. The 2024 amendments introduced a mandatory data-breach-notification obligation and the Data Protection Officer requirement. Cross-border transfer regime is also engaged when the CAAC proxy PoP is outside MY. **Verify against the gazetted Act A1709 and PDPC guidelines; do not state thresholds from this review.**
   - **GDPR Art. 5/6/13/35** — lawful basis for routing employee browser sessions through an inspection proxy; Art. 35 DPIA almost certainly required for SSL-MITM-equivalent on workforce traffic, Art. 88 / Member-State employment-law lens.
   - **EDPB Guidelines 05/2020 on consent in employment** — consent is not a valid lawful basis where there is a power imbalance; legitimate interest balancing test required.
   - **HKPCPD employer-monitoring guidance** — disclosure-before-monitoring expected.
   - **MAS TRM and BNM RMiT** require monitoring tooling respect privacy obligations; neither overrides PDPA.
   The CAAC story is *less* invasive than a forward-proxy SSL-bump because it only sees sanctioned-app sessions routed via the IdP — that is a mitigation worth stating. But the draft never makes the argument. Make it explicit, or HR/Legal/DPO will block the deployment.

4. **DSPM-for-AI / GenAI prompt visibility (line 101) is the highest-risk privacy item in the draft and is undisclosed.** Microsoft Purview DSPM for AI surfaces **the actual prompts and responses** submitted to ChatGPT Enterprise / Gemini / supported AI sites for sensitive-data inspection (line 101). The compliance treatment:
   - The prompt text is personal data when it contains a user identifier or first-person content; the response may be personal data of a third party (someone the user is asking about).
   - This is high-risk processing under GDPR Art. 35 — DPIA mandatory.
   - PDPA MY 2024-amendment cross-border transfer treatment must be documented if Purview's DSPM-for-AI evaluation happens outside MY.
   - Admin access to prompt content is itself a privileged-access-management control (NIST CSF PR.AA-05; ISO 27001 A.5.18).
   - The draft says "DSPM-for-AI prompt visibility requires Purview-onboarded devices (Defender for Endpoint)" (line 101) — but never says the prompts themselves are stored, by whom, for how long, with what access control. **Treat as the dominant privacy exposure of this product; require DPO sign-off before any pilot.**

5. **Regulator clocks are entirely absent.** None of the following are mentioned, all of which a CASB practitioner would be expected to know:
   - **BNM RMiT** — material technology / cyber incident reporting; outsourcing/third-party arrangement notifications (verify clock against gazetted document and current BNM circulars).
   - **MAS TRM / MAS Notice on Technology Risk Management** — incident reporting expectations and root-cause submission windows (verify against current Notice text).
   - **HKMA SA-2 and the General Principles for Technology Risk Management** — incident reporting expectations.
   - **PDPA MY 2024 amendments** — breach-notification clock as gazetted; DPO requirement; cross-border transfer regime.
   - **GDPR Art. 33** — 72-hour notification to supervisory authority.
   - **EU DORA** — major-ICT-incident classification and RTS reporting (relevant if the FI has EU footprint, including via M365 multi-geo).
   - **PCI DSS v4.0 Req. 12.10** — incident response; for a tenant where Defender for Cloud Apps gates cardholder-data flow, the CASB events become PCI evidence and must be retained for 12 months minimum (PCI DSS Req. 10.5.1).
   A CASB is the **detection** and often the **evidence source** for these clocks. The draft never says which Defender XDR alert class starts which clock, nor whether Defender's timestamps and policy-version fields are reliable enough to defend a notification window. **Add a clocks section before promotion; cite primary sources only.**

6. **API-mode policies are after-the-fact — the draft acknowledges latency only for Power BI / Dynamics 365 (line 327) but never draws the consequence for the breach clock.** Policy #2 (auto-label PCI), Policy #4 (mass-download), Policy #6 (OAuth revoke), Policy #10 (quarantine externally-shared), Policy #11 (malware on Box/Dropbox/Google), Policy #12 (terminated-user activity) are all API-mode. M365 connector latency is typically <15 minutes for SharePoint/OneDrive but unbounded for Power BI/Dynamics (24–72 hours, line 327). An auditor will ask: (a) documented MTTR for each API policy; (b) MTTR vs. regulator breach clock; (c) compensating inline control for the pre-detection window. The draft offers none.

7. **Risk scoring (0–10) is vendor-proprietary and not independently corroborated (line 49).** The Cloud App Catalog risk score is the gating condition in Policy #7 (auto-unsanction GenAI above risk threshold, line 188). The draft says "90+ factors across General, Security, Compliance, Legal categories" but states the score is "Microsoft's opinion, not an industry standard; weights are not user-tunable" (line 49). An auditor cannot accept a black-box risk score as the basis for an automated enforcement decision. ISO 27017 CLD.6.3.1 (shared responsibility) and ISO 27036 (supplier relationships) expect documented risk methodology, not vendor opinion. NIST CSF GV.SC-04 (suppliers known and prioritised by criticality) expects a documented method, not a vendor scalar. **Treat the catalog risk score as a triage input only; document a parallel internal risk-acceptance process for auto-unsanction decisions.**

8. **The "Misleading publisher name" anomaly (Policy #6, line 175) is graded false-positive risk "Low" with no operational basis.** "Microsoft's catalogue+publisher-verification filter is reasonably tight" (line 185) is a vendor-trust statement, not evidence. Without a measured FP rate and a documented review process for the auto-revoke action, the policy will either (a) auto-revoke a legitimate but lookalike-named ISV consent and break business flow, or (b) miss a malicious lookalike because the publisher-verified flag was social-engineered. An auditor will require the FP/FN baseline before accepting auto-revoke as a control; "vendor confidence" is not it. NIST CSF DE.AE-02 (analyse event data) expects the rate basis.

9. **"50 file-policy hard limit per tenant" (line 324) is a control-design constraint the draft mentions but does not size.** Most regulated FIs have more than 50 distinct sensitive-data classes × app combinations they want to govern (cardholder data × OneDrive, cardholder × SharePoint, cardholder × Teams, KYC × OneDrive, KYC × SharePoint, ... ≥ 30 just for the M365 stack at any mid-sized bank). The "consolidate" guidance pushes practitioners toward broader policies, which dilutes evidentiary precision (one policy ID fires across many content classes, weakening audit traceability). **Document the policy-consolidation strategy as a control-design decision with explicit risk acceptance.**

10. **OAuth coverage gap (line 319) is acknowledged but not weighted.** The draft says only *Delegated* OAuth permissions are surfaced; *Application* (client-credentials / app-only) grants need Defender XDR / app governance / Defender for Cloud (line 319). For a regulated FI, app-only consents are the higher-risk class (service principals run unattended, often with broad mailbox/file scopes, often used for data exfil staging). NIST CSF PR.AA-05 and ISO 27001 A.5.16 (identity management) both expect coverage of non-human identities. **Policy #6 cannot evidence comprehensive OAuth governance until app-only consents are covered. State this as a residual risk.**

11. **No retention, no integrity, no chain-of-custody on logs.** The draft never states (a) Defender XDR Advanced Hunting default retention (30 days standard, longer with add-on / Sentinel forwarding), (b) maximum retention available, (c) whether records are tamper-evident at rest, (d) what hash/signing covers SIEM export, (e) whether Microsoft can produce a forensically defensible log extract on subpoena / Production Order. BNM RMiT logging/audit-trail expectations (current edition), ISO 27017 CLD.12.4.5 (monitoring of cloud services), and PCI DSS Req. 10.5 (protect audit trails / Req. 10.7 retention) all require this. Without it the SIEM export is a convenience feature, not evidence.

12. **Defender for Cloud Apps tenant region is "paraphrased from general practitioner knowledge" (line 358).** This is exactly the wrong place for a paraphrase. For an MY FI under BNM RMiT, an SG FI under MAS TRM/Cloud Advisory, an EU controller under GDPR Chapter V, the **storage region of CASB metadata, DLP incident snippets, and UEBA telemetry** is a cross-border-data-transfer determination. **Pull the current authoritative list of Defender for Cloud Apps primary-data geos from the Microsoft Trust Center / Service Trust Portal and cite the document URL and accessed date. Until done, do not propose this product to a residency-sensitive MY/SG/HK FI.**

13. **Sub-processor disclosure (ISO 27018 A.10/A.11) absent.** Microsoft publishes the Online Services Sub-processor List via the Microsoft Trust Center; the draft does not reference it. ISO 27018 A.11.1 requires the cloud-PII-processor to disclose sub-processors and notify customers of changes. **Cite the current sub-processor list URL and the notification mechanism (six-month advance notification is Microsoft's standard).**

14. **No third-party attestation citations.** A GRC reference cannot promote a vendor without naming the attestations the buyer will rely on for shared-responsibility evidence. The draft cites Microsoft Learn product documentation 16 times and zero attestation reports. State (each with report date and current scope, pulled from the Microsoft Trust Center / Service Trust Portal):
   - **SOC 2 Type II** scope for Defender for Cloud Apps.
   - **ISO/IEC 27001:2022** scope (Defender for Cloud Apps should be in-scope under the broader Microsoft 365 / Azure certification).
   - **ISO/IEC 27017** and **ISO/IEC 27018** attestation coverage.
   - **CSA STAR Level 2** attestation.
   - **FedRAMP** authorisation level (Defender for Cloud Apps GCC / GCC-High status).
   - **C5** (Germany), **IRAP** (Australia), **MTCS** (Singapore), **OSPAR** (Singapore — banking).
   - **HIPAA BAA** availability.
   Mark each as **Unverified** until pulled from the Microsoft Trust Center; do not assert from memory.

15. **Office 365 Cloud App Security (OCAS) — the subset SKU — is a compliance trap the draft mentions but does not size (line 21, line 361).** A regulated FI buying "Office 365 E5" expecting CASB will get OCAS, which has no cross-SaaS DLP, no third-party connectors, no app governance, no full SSPM. An FI's GRC reliance on "we have CASB via O365 E5" is materially mis-stated. **This belongs in the canonical 04-vendors entry as a procurement / licensing pitfall, not relegated to reviewer notes.**

16. **Step-up auth is in preview (line 97, line 356) — controls in preview are not auditable.** Microsoft "Preview" status does not carry the same SLA, support, or security commitments as GA. Any policy in the draft that relies on step-up auth (mentioned for session policies generally; Policy #1 could compose with it) cannot be promoted as an enforceable control. State the GA-vs-preview status on every policy that uses a preview feature.

17. **The discovered-subdomains deprecation (line 45, line 325) destroys an existing audit trail.** Practitioners who built BNM RMiT / MAS TRM evidence on SharePoint-tenant-level Shadow IT discovery (e.g. distinguishing your own SharePoint Online tenant from a rogue one) will lose that fidelity at 2025-12-31. **Flag as a transition-risk item: control evidence that depended on subdomain differentiation expired in late 2025. Re-baseline before any new compliance attestation cycle.**

## Policies to refine

- **Policy #1 (Block download to unmanaged)** — add: (a) the `CloudAppEvents` action and schema-field list that evidences the block, (b) default and maximum retention, (c) the BNM RMiT / MAS TRM / ISO 27017 control IDs evidenced, (d) the Conditional-Access-policy ID it depends on (state explicitly the audit trail is split across **Entra ID sign-in logs**, **Conditional Access policy result**, and **Defender for Cloud Apps session-policy event** — the auditor needs all three correlated), (e) regulator-notification decision tree when the file blocked was regulated content.
- **Policy #2 (Auto-label PCI)** — clarify storage and access control on the matching-content preview snippet (it is itself cardholder data under PCI DSS 3.4/3.5); state Purview label dependency as a precondition; specify the lawful basis for content inspection of OneDrive personal-storage areas (some tenants treat OneDrive as personal — PDPA / GDPR exposure).
- **Policy #3 (Block upload of unlabelled PII)** — add the DPIA reference; "False-positive risk: High" (line 147) acknowledged but no mitigation plan stated. Specify: SIT-by-SIT confidence-level threshold, the audit log of false-positive overrides, and whether the per-user override is itself evidenced.
- **Policy #4 (Mass-download anomaly)** — state the 1,000-in-30-min threshold as a documented control parameter under change management; specify service-account exclusion list as a configuration item with named owner; specify how the Entra "Suspend user" governance action is logged (which directory event, which actor field).
- **Policy #5 (Block sign-in from non-compliant device)** — "False-positive risk: High — breaks BYOD entirely" (line 173) acknowledged. State explicitly: this policy fails BYOD by design; if BYOD is in scope, a separate access policy with read-only session controls is mandatory. Specify the device-claim evidence (Entra-hybrid-joined assertion source, Intune-compliant claim source, freshness).
- **Policy #6 (OAuth revoke)** — split into "Detect" (NIST CSF DE.CM-09) and "Respond" (RS.MI-02); state that Application-permission OAuth grants are out of scope (line 319) and require Defender XDR / app governance as a compensating control; add the revoke-evidence log artefact.
- **Policy #7 (Auto-unsanction GenAI by risk threshold)** — make the risk-score threshold (≤5) a documented control parameter under change management; specify the appeal / exception process (an FI's approved AI vendor may score ≤5 due to "SOC 2 = No" while still being approved); specify the MDE-managed-device blocking dependency as a precondition (without MDE, the unsanction is informational only); document the lawful basis for blocking employee access to GenAI (employment-context — usually OK, but document).
- **Policy #8 (Block clipboard paste to ChatGPT)** — the "Practitioner-named" tag on line 211 means this is not a Microsoft template; it depends on the ChatGPT app being onboarded via SAML federation. State the precondition explicitly. Specify the privacy treatment: clipboard-content inspection on the user's browser is high-intrusion processing; require DPIA. Specify the audit-log content (is the cut/copy/paste action logged with the truncated content snippet? if so, that's another regulated-content store).
- **Policy #9 (Impossible travel)** — already a built-in policy with documented sensitivity slider, but the draft does not specify which user-attribute (location, ASN, ISP) is logged in the alert; specify and tie to PDPA / GDPR processing notice (this is workforce-location processing).
- **Policy #10 (Quarantine externally-shared, no recent activity)** — add documented MTTR target; map MTTR to breach-notification clocks; state how legal-hold / e-discovery interacts with quarantine action (a quarantined file on legal hold may be spoliation); specify the "Last modified > 180 days" baseline and change management.
- **Policy #11 (Malware on Box / Dropbox / Google)** — state sandbox jurisdiction (where the file is detonated); for a regulated FI, that location matters under outsourcing rules; clarify "doesn't auto-block" (line 89) as a residual-risk acceptance.
- **Policy #12 (Terminated-user activity)** — depends on Entra as IdP (line 261); state explicitly. Specify the audit-log artefact for the cross-app activity correlation; specify the manual-revoke evidence flow for AWS/GCP/Salesforce.

## Limitations the draft missed

- **No breach-notification-clock decision tree.** When does a Defender for Cloud Apps alert become a notifiable incident under BNM RMiT / MAS TRM / GDPR Art. 33 / PDPA 2024? Who decides? Where is the decision logged? CASB is upstream of this workflow; the draft pretends the workflow doesn't exist.
- **No legal-hold / e-discovery interaction with quarantine.** Policy #10 trashes / quarantines files. If those files are subject to a Microsoft Purview eDiscovery hold or a regulator notice, the quarantine may be spoliation. Microsoft Purview eDiscovery preservation interacts with Defender for Cloud Apps quarantine — state the rule (Purview hold wins, but the operator may not know about the hold at policy-design time).
- **No data-subject-rights (DSR) interaction.** Under PDPA MY (Section 30 access rights, 2024 amendment scope), GDPR Art. 15–22, HKPCPD DPP 6 — a data subject can request access/erasure of personal data Defender holds about them (their browsing, their CAAC sessions, their DLP hits, their DSPM-for-AI prompts). How does Microsoft service that request? Not mentioned.
- **No DPIA / TIA scaffolding.** CAAC reverse-proxy of employee browser sessions and DSPM-for-AI prompt visibility are both high-risk processing under GDPR Art. 35; EU→non-EU transfer of incident metadata requires a Transfer Impact Assessment post-Schrems II. Not mentioned.
- **No Microsoft-own incident-response and customer-notification SLA.** If Microsoft is breached such that Defender tenant data is exposed, when does the customer hear? Microsoft Online Services DPA contains the SLA; the draft does not cite it. This is BNM RMiT third-party / MAS TRM outsourcing concern.
- **No concentration-risk statement.** Defender for Cloud Apps couples to Entra ID, Defender XDR, Microsoft Purview, Conditional Access, MDE, and (optionally) Global Secure Access. A regulated FI running M365 + Defender + Entra + Purview is single-vendor for IdP, EDR, CASB, DLP, and SSE — a textbook concentration-risk pattern under BNM RMiT third-party / MAS Outsourcing / EU DORA concentration-risk recital. Promote with this risk named.
- **No exit / portability statement.** When the FI moves off Defender for Cloud Apps, can it export policies, file-policy definitions, DLP signatures, UEBA baselines, historical logs? In a defensible format? ISO 27017 CLD.8.1.5 and BNM RMiT outsourcing exit-strategy expectations both bite.
- **No tenant-data encryption / key-management statement.** Logs, DLP incident metadata, DSPM-for-AI prompt content — encryption-at-rest? Microsoft-managed key only? Customer Key (Microsoft Purview Customer Key) available for the Defender data stores or not? BYOK / HYOK not addressed.
- **No availability / SLA statement.** Defender for Cloud Apps inline session-policy fail-mode (fail-open vs fail-closed) is a security and compliance decision. If the CAAC proxy is unreachable, do sessions block or pass through? Not mentioned.
- **No regulator inspection-rights statement.** BNM RMiT and MAS Outsourcing require the regulator and the FI to retain audit / inspection rights against the cloud service provider. Microsoft's audit-rights position is documented in the DPA / Financial Services Amendment — cite it.
- **No Customer Lockbox / admin-access transparency.** When Microsoft engineering needs to access Defender tenant data for a support case, does Customer Lockbox apply? This is material to ISO 27018 A.10.13 (return, transfer, and disposal of PII) and A.9.4.1 (access control to program source code-equivalent for tenant data).
- **No statement on Microsoft Defender XDR data correlation outside the FI's region.** Defender for Cloud Apps incidents correlate with Defender for Endpoint, Defender for Office 365, Defender for Identity. The cross-product correlation may pull data from a different geographic tenant scope than Defender for Cloud Apps' primary-data region. State the cross-product data-flow.
- **No statement on M365 multi-geo (line 328) compliance implications.** The draft says SharePoint multi-geo events "aren't fully captured" — for an MY FI with a Singapore SharePoint geo-allocation, this means the CASB coverage for SG-geo content is partial. State the residual risk.
- **No statement on the personal-Microsoft-account (MSA) carve-out.** If an employee signs into chat.openai.com with a personal Microsoft account, Entra ID does not see it, CAAC does not see it, Policy #8 does not fire. Same for personal Gmail used to sign in to ChatGPT (line 318 acknowledges the gap but doesn't state the compliance consequence).
- **No statement on shared-responsibility for the Microsoft Defender XDR Schema.** When Microsoft changes the `CloudAppEvents` schema (it has, multiple times), in-place SIEM queries and audit reports may silently break. State the change-management practice (subscribe to the schema-change blog; freeze schema-dependent queries).

## Overall verdict

- **Promote as-is to 04-vendors/?** NO
- **One-line reason:** Strong product-level draft, but evidence-thin for GRC — no control mapping, no log-artefact specification, no regulator-clock treatment, no privacy/DPIA treatment of CAAC routing or DSPM-for-AI prompt capture, no third-party attestation citations, no sub-processor / residency citations. Rewrite with the above before promotion.
